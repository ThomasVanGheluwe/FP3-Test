Where the story began.

On the 6th of July, John Lennon is playing with his band 'The Quarrymen' during a small festival in Woolton, Liverpool. He then met Paul McCartney in between performances, who in that same year joined the band. McCartney brought in his friend George Harrison only a few months later although he was very young at the time (only 14). And so the foundation of The Beatles was laid.

The Quarrymen

The Quarrymen were much like the Beatles, a British popband founded in 1956 by John Lennon and joined by Paul and George in 1957. The band was named after a small gymnasium, 'Quarry Bank High School' in Liverpool.

The original band consisted out of Pete Shotton, Eric Griffiths, Bill Smith (bas) en Rod Davis, Len Garry en Colin Hanson.

When the Beatles became the Beatles.

It was in 1960 when The Beatles first became "The Beatles", a name made up by Stuart Sutcliffe (member until 1961). However more changes followed, even being named "Silver Beetles" for about a month.
Finally in August 1960, the band was crowned as "The Beatles" a clever pun derived from beat and beetles (insect) which is also an hommage to their supporting band "The Crickets".

Early performances (60'-62').

At first, The Beatles were regular players at the Jacaranda club, Slaterstreet Liverpool. This is how Allan Williams became their first manager who later scored them gigs in Hamburg.

Without them knowing it on October 15th of 1960 The Beatles played with their final line-up for the first time (Ringo Starr played the drums during this session). This where they first recorded "Summertime".

Summertime
The Beatles, Hamburg 1960.

In November 1960, George Harrison is deported from Hamburg for being a minor without a working permit. By December The Beatles had all returned to Liverpool although they later return to Hamburg in 1961.

1961 is when the ball first really started rolling, singles like "Love me do" and "She loves you" started gaining attention. Their B-side records often achieved A-side hit-potential.

Beatlemania in town!

By 1963 the Beatles had grown so successful in the UK a mass hysterical following had erupted among British youngsters. The press quickly coined the term "Beatlemania".

However it was only until the end of the year the band started getting traction outside of the UK, specifically Sweden. The Beatles' surge in Australia, the US, Asia and the rest of Europe only started around January 1964.

"Please please me" was their album that went around at that time.

Pop band/Moviestars

In 1964 the Beatles expanded their medium and started making their own black and white films. Like the black and white comedy "A hard day's night", partly documentary. "A hard day's night" was even one of the first rock movies that was well received by film critics. Since then the movie has become a cult-film for Beatles fans.

Other movies released by the Beatles include: "Help!" (1965), "Magical Mystery Tour" (1967), "Yellow Submarine" (1968) en "Let It Be" (1970).

By the end 1964 the heavy touring and travelling resulted in the Beatles taking a deep dive in creativity and fatigue becoming noticeable in their music. It is said that on the album "The Beatles For Sale" their tiredness is most noticeable. Their lack of artistic movement however was compensated by the commercial succes.

By 1965 however their creative talents surged 'high' once again under the influence of marijuana, introduced to them by Bob Dylan.

"Rubber Soul" and a hard 1966

At the end of 1965 the Beatles released their "Rubber Soul" album. The record marks the transition from the Beatles' debut to a period of renewing music. It's the first record including only Beatles songs and the band name is not mentioned on the album cover.

Mounting time constraints and the fact that the more and more sophisticated Ip's could no longer be produced live because of screaming fans, the Beatles stopped touring in 1966.

Other controversies

While in America, John Lennon sparked quite the controversy when he claimed the Beatles to be more popular than Jezus.

But their travels in Asia were not much smoother, in the Philippines the band were harassed by mobs after declining breakfast with the president's wife Imelda Marcos. In Japan they were "treated like prisoners".

The beginning of the studio years

After "Rubber Soul" and the ending of live performing, two more albums were created. While touring, the album "Revolver" was created but was never really played live. After this album the Beatles switched over to studio recording, for another album: "Sgt. Pepper's lonely hearts band".

This last album is considered by many to be the absolute peak in their artistic career.

The beginning of the end

The Beatles have existed for nine years at this point in time, for everyone except Ringo it's been twelve already. Their most recent double album, "The Beatles" was released in 68', with much difficulty and tension.

A last attempt at the Beatles

Paul McCartney made an attempt at return to the simplicity and spirit of the old Beatles with the "Get-Back"-project. The project would feature a small tour, a movie about the tour, a single and an album. However tension remain present and keep rising among the band members. Eventually, the project fails, no tour takes place and no album is released.

Despite that, an unannounced live performance is given on the 30th of January on the roof of an Apple office in London, but is shut down by local law-enforcement after about 42 minutes of playing. Their was no album of course but a single "Get Back" was released.

Get back
The Beatles, 1969.

Down Abbey Road, for good

The Beatles' last successful ep, "Abbey Road" was released in September 1969. The members had put their differences aside to deliver one final album. The last (intended) song on the album was revealingly called "The End".

Not much Later John Lennon, founder of the Beatles announced to his co-members that he was quitting the band.
